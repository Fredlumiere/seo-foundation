Where the site stands today

Every page on tmpc.org was scored on four things a normal visitor actually cares about: how fast it loads, how easy it is for everyone to use (including people with vision or mobility challenges), whether it follows modern web best practices, and how well Google can read it. Scores are out of 100.

The short version

The site is technically well-built (great Best Practices & SEO scores) but it is slow and hard to use for people with disabilities. Most of this comes from two things: images that are too big, and missing labels on images, links, and form fields.

How to read this PDF. Each fix is on its own page. They're ordered by importance — start at #1 and go down. Every fix tells you: what's wrong, which pages have it, and step-by-step what to click in SnapPages to fix it. Blue underlined text is clickable.

What you'll need

  • SnapPages login — the editor for tmpc.org pages
  • TinyPNG.com — free image compressor (no account needed)
  • About 3-4 hours total, spread over a few sessions

Shrink the homepage images

CriticalHome page~45 min
tmpc.org — 29.7s load, 29 MB total weight

Also slow on these pages

  • /
  • /linktree
  • /students
  • /volunteer
  • /bulletin

What's wrong

The homepage weighs 29 megabytes — about the size of a movie trailer. On a typical phone connection, visitors wait almost 30 seconds to see the main image. Google measures this and downranks slow sites. Many visitors won't wait and will leave.

The fix is almost entirely one thing: the big photos on the homepage are saved at print-quality resolution instead of web resolution. A 5 MB photo looks identical to a 300 KB photo on a screen — but loads 15× faster.

Step-by-step in SnapPages

  1. Go to tinypng.com in a new tab. Leave it open — you'll drag images into it.
  2. Log into SnapPages and open the Home page in the editor.
  3. Click the big hero image at the top. Click Replace → Download original. Save to Desktop.
  4. Drag that file onto TinyPNG. Wait a few seconds. Click Download — the new file is 70-90% smaller.
  5. Back in SnapPages, click Replace → Upload → choose the compressed file.
  6. Repeat for every photo on the Home page. There are roughly 6-8 big ones.
  7. Click Publish at the top right.
Quick check: After publishing, open pagespeed.web.dev/report?url=tmpc.org. Speed score should jump from 30 to 60+.

Add alt text to every image

Critical56 of 57 pages~60 min
Every page has this problem — example: /about

How big is the problem

56 of 57 pages have at least one image with no alt text. Start with the most-visited pages:

  • /
  • /about
  • /worship
  • /give
  • /sermons

What's wrong

Every image on a website needs a short description called "alt text." It does two jobs: (1) screen readers for blind or low-vision visitors read it out loud so they know what the image shows, and (2) Google uses it to understand what the page is about.

On tmpc.org, most images have no alt text at all. A visitor using a screen reader just hears "image" — nothing about whether it's a photo of Sunday worship, the pastor, or the building.

Step-by-step in SnapPages

  1. Open any page in the SnapPages editor.
  2. Click on an image. A toolbar appears.
  3. Click the Settings / gear icon on the image.
  4. Find the field labeled Alt text (sometimes called "Description").
  5. Type a short, truthful description — aim for 5-10 words. Example: "Congregation singing during Sunday worship" or "Exterior view of the stone church building."
  6. Decorative images (dividers, background shapes) can be skipped or given alt text of "" (empty).
  7. Click Publish.
Tip: Don't start with "Image of..." — screen readers already announce it's an image. Just say what it shows.

Label the social media icon links

Critical56 pages~15 min · template fix
The footer Facebook / Instagram / YouTube icons
Heads up — this one may need Bailey or a SnapPages support ticket. If you can't find an alt text / label field on the footer icons, it's because they're part of the site theme, not a page you can edit.

What's wrong

The Facebook, Instagram, and YouTube icons in the footer are links with no words. Sighted visitors see the icons and know what they mean. But a screen reader user just hears "link, link, link" — no idea what each one does.

This happens on 56 of 57 pages because the footer is the same on every page.

Step-by-step in SnapPages

  1. Log into SnapPages. Go to Site Settings → Footer (or wherever the social icons are configured).
  2. Click each social icon.
  3. Look for a field called Title, Alt text, or Accessibility label.
  4. Fill in: "Facebook", "Instagram", "YouTube" — matching what each one links to.
  5. If there is no such field anywhere, email support@snappages.com with this exact ask:
    "The social media icons in our theme footer are missing accessible labels (aria-label or title). Our accessibility audit is flagging this on every page. How do we add labels?"
  6. Publish.

Critical · SEO

Give each page its own description

Critical10+ key pages~60 min
/inclusivity — same description as homepage

Fix these first

  • /
  • /about
  • /give
  • /inclusivity
  • /mission
  • /addiction-recovery
  • /lent

What's wrong

Every page on the site currently has the same meta description: "Thompson Memorial Presbyterian Church in New Hope, PA — an inclusive, open and affirming church where all are welcome. Sunday worship at 9 & 11 am. Above all, love."

That's fine for the home page. It's bad everywhere else. When someone Googles "New Hope church giving" and sees our /give page in results, they see that same generic text instead of something like "Support TMPC's mission through one-time or recurring gifts." They're less likely to click.

Google also treats duplicate descriptions as a signal that pages aren't distinct — so it often won't show the individual pages at all.

Step-by-step in SnapPages

  1. Open a page in SnapPages (start with Home, then About, Give, etc.).
  2. Click Page Settings (usually a gear icon near the page name at the top).
  3. Find SEO → Meta Description.
  4. Write a unique 140-160 character description of what that specific page is about. Be concrete.
  5. Examples:
    About: "Meet the pastors, learn our Presbyterian roots, and see how TMPC became New Hope's open & affirming church."
    Give: "Support TMPC's ministry in New Hope, PA. One-time or recurring gifts — online, text, or in person."
    Inclusivity: "TMPC is an open & affirming congregation. Read our full inclusivity statement and what it means in practice."
  6. Save, then publish.

Fix form field labels

Important23 pages~30 min
/connect — signup form

Form pages affected

  • /connect
  • /baptism-signup
  • /bible-reading-group-signup
  • /volunteer-signup
  • /community-meal

What's wrong

Some form fields — places where visitors type their name, email, etc. — have no programmatic label. Sighted users see the word "Email" next to the box, but that word isn't connected to the box in code. Screen readers can't announce what each field is for, and autofill on phones often fails.

Most of these forms are embedded from Subsplash, not built in SnapPages directly. That matters for how you fix them.

Step-by-step

  1. If it's a Subsplash form (most signups): log into Subsplash → Forms → open each form → check each field has a Label set (not just placeholder text). Save & republish.
  2. If it's a native SnapPages form (the /connect page may be one): open the page, click each form field, look for a Label field in the settings panel. Fill it in ("First name", "Email address", etc.).
  3. If you're not sure whether it's SnapPages or Subsplash, look at the form visually — Subsplash forms usually look more polished and have a blue submit button.
  4. Publish.

Fix low-contrast text

Important17 pages~20 min
/give — faint text on light backgrounds

Pages with contrast issues

  • /
  • /about
  • /bulletin
  • /community-meal
  • /fall-fest

What's wrong

Some text on the site is too light against its background to pass accessibility standards. Common culprits: light gray body text on white, gold/tan text on cream backgrounds, and white captions on bright photos.

This isn't a style preference — it fails the WCAG 2.1 AA standard that churches (and everyone else) are legally expected to meet. Older members and anyone in bright sunlight on a phone struggle to read it.

Step-by-step in SnapPages

  1. Open each page listed on the left.
  2. Click any text that looks faint. Look at the color setting (usually in the text toolbar).
  3. If the color is a light gray like #999, change it to a darker gray like #555 or full black #000.
  4. For text sitting on a photo, either (a) use a darker overlay behind the text, or (b) move the text to a solid-color band above/below the photo.
  5. Check with this free tool: webaim.org/resources/contrastchecker — paste the two colors in, it tells you pass/fail.
  6. Publish.

Stop the page from jumping as it loads

Important12 pages~15 min
Home page has the worst layout shift

Pages that jump

  • /
  • /bulletin
  • /calendar
  • /connect
  • /files
  • /linktree

What's wrong

You've seen this before: you start reading a page, then an image loads and shoves everything else down so you lose your place — or worse, you accidentally tap the wrong button because it moved. That's called layout shift.

It happens when images load without the page knowing how big they'll be, so the page reserves no space for them up front. When they arrive, they push everything down.

Step-by-step

  1. This one is partly solved by fixing #1 (smaller images load faster, so less shifting is visible).
  2. In SnapPages, for any image block with manual custom code: make sure each <img> has width and height attributes set.
  3. For Subsplash embeds (calendar, bulletin, files): in the embed settings, look for a "Fixed height" or "Min-height" option and set it. That prevents the embed from growing as it loads.
  4. Publish, then reload the page with Cmd+Shift+R — watch whether things stay put.
Reality check: Some of this lives inside the SnapPages theme code and isn't fixable in the editor. If it doesn't improve after #1, put this on Bailey's list to raise with SnapPages support.

Fix heading structure

Nice to have6 pages~20 min
/building and /connect are typical offenders

Pages affected

  • /building
  • /connect
  • /events
  • /kids
  • /lent
  • /what-we-believe

What's wrong

Every page has headings (big text, smaller subheads, etc.) organized in levels: Heading 1 (the page title), Heading 2 (major sections), Heading 3 (subsections), and so on. These aren't just visual — screen readers use them as a table of contents.

On these 6 pages, the levels skip around — for example jumping from Heading 1 straight to Heading 3, skipping Heading 2. That confuses both screen readers and Google.

Step-by-step in SnapPages

  1. Open one of the pages on the left.
  2. Click each heading/title on the page. In the text formatting toolbar, check the current level (H1, H2, H3…).
  3. Rule: only one H1 per page (the main title). Everything else should be H2 for major sections, H3 for subsections, and so on in order — no skipping.
  4. If something looks like a heading but is styled bold instead, it should probably be a real heading — change it.
  5. Publish.

Shrink the Staff page

Nice to have/staff~30 min
/staff — the largest page at 52 KB HTML + many headshots
Link: tmpc.org/staff

What's wrong

The Staff page is the largest page on the site. It's not broken — the Performance score is 61 — but it has the most room to improve because of all the headshot images.

Same story as Fix #1: each headshot is probably saved at too high a resolution for what's displayed (small circles).

Step-by-step

  1. Same process as Fix #1 — download each headshot, compress on tinypng.com, re-upload.
  2. Bonus: if the headshots display at roughly 300×300 pixels on screen, resize the originals to 600×600 before uploading (Mac Preview → Tools → Adjust Size). That way even retina screens get sharp images without paying for 4000×4000 originals.
  3. Publish.

Fix the Linktree image shape

Nice to have/linktree~5 min
One image on /linktree is stretched
Link: tmpc.org/linktree

What's wrong

One image on the /linktree page is being displayed at a different aspect ratio than the original — it's either squashed or stretched. Visually it looks slightly off; to a designer it screams amateur.

Step-by-step in SnapPages

  1. Open the Linktree page in the editor.
  2. Look at each image in the list. Find the one that looks squashed or stretched compared to the others.
  3. Click the image. Either (a) re-size it by dragging the corners until it looks natural, or (b) upload a replacement cropped to the right shape.
  4. Publish.
